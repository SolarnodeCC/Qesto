# Qesto Brand Voice — Comprehensive Guide

**Audience for this guide:** marketing, content, product, and i18n teams writing for all customer-facing pages — homepage, features, solutions, use cases, and templates.

**Anchor page (canonical voice expression):** `/features/privacy`.

**Strategic direction:** "Bring people in" — the brand should sound like a trusted partner for people who guide groups, not a feature-led software vendor.

---

## Core Brand Promise

**We help people who hold the room — teachers, trainers, facilitators, managers, hosts — feel confident and capable while making participation feel easy and natural for everyone.**

We are not a technical platform. We are a partner in the social and emotional job of making groups work. Our promise is not "more interactivity." It is "lower the social barrier to participation" and "help hosts feel they can read and lead the room."

---

## Voice in one sentence
Confident, evidence-first, never breathless. Sentences earn their length. *Human first, room-aware, and always serving the host's emotional and social need, not just the technical one.*

## What we sound like
- **Human first.** Talk about people, rooms, moments, and outcomes. Not features, not data, not technical architecture.
- **Practical over poetic.** Specific verbs, concrete nouns, real numbers. "See what your audience is thinking" not "Unlock advanced engagement workflows."
- **Peer-to-peer.** We address professionals who already know their craft (HR ops, facilitators, event producers, L&D leads, board secretaries) — we don't explain their job back to them.
- **Room-aware.** We talk about what happens in the room: confidence, inclusion, energy, clarity, contribution. We mention anonymity modes, consent logs, and Workers AI because they *enable* these room outcomes, not because they are technically interesting.
- **Supportive, not instructional.** Help people feel capable, not like they need to become performers.
- **Quietly confident.** No exclamation points, no superlatives, no "revolutionary". Sound assured without sounding distant.
- **Energizing, not noisy.** Lift the mood without becoming gimmicky or hype-driven.
- **Audience-centered.** Focus on what the host and participants *experience*, not what the product *does*.

## What we don't sound like
- **A tech vendor.** Not "Our platform delivers real-time feedback engine capabilities." Instead: "See what your audience is thinking while the moment still matters."
- **A hype machine.** No "revolutionary," "game-changing," "next-generation," or "supercharge."
- **Jargon-heavy.** Not "audience engagement platform," "interaction layer," "real-time feedback engine," "data-driven participation," "response capture," "audience intelligence," "engagement optimization," "integrated polling architecture," "participation workflows," "interaction engine," "optimization layer."
- **Feature-stacking.** Not "Our solution includes live polls, Q&A, rankings, surveys, and AI-powered insights." Instead: "Ask one question and see what your audience is thinking."
- **Empty empathy.** Not "We get it — meetings are hard." Skip the false comfort. Show the failure mode in one sentence and move to what we do: "Silence after you ask a question. Now you can invite people to share what they think, safely."
- **"AI-powered" alone.** Always anchor AI mentions to *what runs where* (Workers AI on Cloudflare's edge).
- **Marketing filler:** *seamless, leverage, best-in-class, world-class, cutting-edge, revolutionary, next-generation, game-changing, empower (as filler), synergy, unleash*.
- **Vague metric tiles:** a tile that says "Higher" with no defensible note should be replaced with a real number or removed.
- **Em-dash for emphasis.** We use em-dashes for parenthetical asides only.

## Numbers rule
Every number on the page must be defensible to a procurement reviewer. If it isn't, do one of three things:
1. Replace with a measurable claim ("`<90s` brief-to-draft" instead of "Faster").
2. Label the tile clearly: `Illustrative target` or `Internal benchmark`.
3. Drop the tile.

## CTA voice (verb-first, context-specific)

**Principles:**
- **Verb first.** Lead with the action. "Launch your next session." "Review the consent model." "Run your first pulse." "See how anonymity works."
- **No exclamation points.**
- **Context-specific.** Match the job to be done and the persona.
  - Homepage: "Bring people into your next session."
  - HR/Team: "See the anonymity modes." or "Hear honest input."
  - Events: "Run your next event with participation."
  - Privacy: "Read the privacy policy."
  - Nonprofit/Governance: "Talk to the governance team."
  - General feature: "Try it now." or "See how it works."
- **Avoid generic CTAs:** No "Learn more," "Click here," "Get started," or "Explore" alone.
- **Make it about the room, not the sign-up:** Not "Sign up for free." Instead: "Launch your first session."

## The 4 messaging pillars (organize content around these)

Every page should anchor to at least one pillar. Feature and solution pages should represent all 4 across the website.

### 1. Bring people in
**Promise:** Participation should feel easy and natural.
**Audience sentiment:** "I need people to join in fast, not sit in silence."
**How to write:** Lead with reducing barriers. "Make it easy for everyone to share what they think." Show the simplicity, the speed of setup, the low stakes for participants.
**Examples:** Homepage, feature overview, onboarding flow.

### 2. Hear every voice
**Promise:** Not just the loudest voices.
**Audience sentiment:** "I need input from people who would never raise their hand."
**How to write:** Emphasize inclusion and psychological safety. Show how people feel safer contributing when there's less pressure (anonymous, easier to type than speak, no spotlight). "Get honest input from people who normally stay quiet." Focus on the safety, not the technical mechanism.
**Examples:** Privacy feature page, HR solutions, workshop use case.

### 3. Keep the room with you
**Promise:** Help hosts feel confident and responsive.
**Audience sentiment:** "I need to read the room and feel like I can adapt."
**How to write:** Focus on host confidence and control. "See what your audience is thinking while the moment still matters." Real-time feedback, the ability to adjust on the fly, looking modern without looking gimmicky.
**Examples:** Live polling feature, team meetings use case, trainer solutions.

### 4. Turn moments into momentum
**Promise:** Move people from passive listening to shared involvement.
**Audience sentiment:** "I want the session to feel like a conversation, not a broadcast."
**How to write:** Show how interaction shifts energy and engagement. "Watch the room come alive." Results, quick outcomes, visibility of shared contribution.
**Examples:** Event hosting, workshop facilitation, training delivery.

## Audience personas & how to speak to each

**Use these personas to shape headline focus, pain point language, and CTA specificity:**

### Teachers & Educators (K–12, Higher Ed)
- **Their pressure:** Keep students with you. Make passive listeners active. Check understanding before moving on.
- **Pain points:** Distraction. Unequal participation. Not knowing if learning stuck.
- **How to write:** Lead with "Active learning" research. Mention checking understanding, visibility of thinking. CTAs: "See what students understand." "Run a quick check."
- **Example page:** TrainingPage.tsx

### Trainers & L&D Professionals
- **Their pressure:** Make training land. Prove it was worth the time. Keep energy high. Build skills, not just compliance.
- **Pain points:** Passive attendance. Low energy. Uneven participation. Boring.
- **How to write:** Lead with learning outcomes and energy. Mention keeping attention, real-time feedback, adjusting on the fly. CTAs: "Run a more engaging training." "See learning in real time."
- **Example page:** TrainingPage.tsx, Launchpad.tsx

### Workshop Facilitators & Co-Creation Leaders
- **Their pressure:** Get everyone to contribute. Surface themes fast. Build momentum without forcing it.
- **Pain points:** Dominant voices. Good ideas lost. Sessions feel flat. Awkward starts.
- **How to write:** Lead with co-creation, inclusion, and momentum. "Get everyone in the room." Mention anonymity, idea surfacing, real-time themes. CTAs: "See how to include everyone." "Try a ranking question."
- **Example page:** WorkshopsPage.tsx

### Event Hosts & Conference Organizers
- **Their pressure:** Make the audience feel part of the event, not just present. Create energy at scale. Collect Q&A.
- **Pain points:** Passive audience. One-way delivery. Disconnection. Dead air.
- **How to write:** Lead with audience experience and energy. "Turn your audience into participants." Mention live Q&A, real-time feedback, memorable moments. CTAs: "Run your next event with participation."
- **Example page:** EventsPage.tsx

### Team Leads & Managers
- **Their pressure:** Get honest input. Build psychological safety. Create alignment. Make people feel heard.
- **Pain points:** Silence. Only confident people speak. Shallow feedback. Fear of speaking up.
- **How to write:** Lead with honest input and safety. "Make all-hands feel like conversations." Mention anonymity, privacy, inclusivity. CTAs: "See the anonymity modes." "Hear honest input."
- **Example page:** HRPage.tsx, TeamMeetingsPage.tsx

### Consultants & External Facilitators
- **Their pressure:** Look polished and adaptive. Show modern facilitation skill. Gather insights fast. Build client confidence.
- **Pain points:** Client skepticism. Low participation. Sessions that feel too theoretical.
- **How to write:** Lead with facilitation capability and polish. "Show modern facilitation." Mention speed of setup, reliability in live moments, visible skill. CTAs: "See how consultants use it." "Try a live session."
- **Example page:** ConsultingPage.tsx

### Community Builders & Meeting Organizers
- **Their pressure:** Build belonging and trust. Encourage contribution. Make people feel welcome.
- **Pain points:** Low trust. Low participation. Only the confident few speak. Awkwardness.
- **How to write:** Lead with connection and inclusion. "Help your community feel heard." Mention accessibility, ease of joining, low barriers. CTAs: "Make meetings more welcoming."
- **Example page:** NonprofitPage.tsx, TeamInvite.tsx

## AI claims (soft mention, business-value-first)

When mentioning AI, focus on what it does for the host — not the technical architecture. Keep it simple and human.

**Principles:**
- Lead with the business outcome, not the technology.
- Mention AI only when it directly helps the host's job.
- Use conversational language; avoid "machine learning," "neural networks," or "advanced algorithms."
- Position AI as a capability that reduces work, not as a feature to show off.

**Example good AI mentions:**
- "Get real-time summaries of what your audience thinks — you don't have to read through every response."
- "See themes surface automatically from the responses you collect."
- "Let AI suggest your next question based on what people said."
- "Get instant feedback analysis while the room is still engaged."

**Example to avoid:**
- ~~"AI-powered insights."~~ (Too vague, sounds like jargon.)
- ~~"Machine learning to optimize engagement."~~ (Technical, not benefit-focused.)
- ~~"Advanced AI analysis."~~ (Makes it sound harder than it is.)
- ~~"Workers AI edge inference."~~ (Completely technical, means nothing to a teacher.)

## Page structure & rhythm (template-aware)

Every page should follow this flow to align messaging with the 4 pillars: Bring people in, Hear every voice, Keep the room with you, Turn moments into momentum.

- **Headline:** Outcome-first, not feature-first. Names the host's job or the room moment. "Help more people join in" not "Live polling features."
- **Hero subheadline:** ≤ 30 words. One promise + one differentiator. Example: "Make it easy for everyone to share what they think and stay engaged from start to finish."

- **Pain points section:** 3 items, ≤ 22 words each. **Each must name a *consequence*, not a feeling.** Not "Low engagement" but "Silence when you ask a question" or "Only the confident people speak up."

- **How-it-works / Features section:** 3–4 items, ≤ 24 words each. **Lead with the verb the *host* does**, not what the product does. "Ask a question → Collect responses → See what people think → Make decisions faster" not "Session created → Data captured → Analysis displayed."

- **Outcome / Proof tiles:** Show what changes in the room. Emotional outcomes: "Quieter people speak up," "The room feels more alive," "You feel more confident." Practical outcomes: "85% participation," "Alignment happens 3x faster." Every number must be defensible; if not, label as "Typical outcome" or remove.
  - Value ≤ 18 chars
  - Label ≤ 30 chars
  - Note ≤ 18 words

- **FAQ answers:** ≤ 80 words, written as if a busy facilitator pasted them into Slack to share with a peer. No jargon.

- **Bottom CTA section:**
  - Heading ≤ 10 words
  - Subheading ≤ 14 words
  - Button: verb-first, context-specific

## SEO hygiene
- **Meta title:** ≤ 60 chars. Primary keyword first. Use outcome language: "Live Polling for Better Meetings" not "Live Polling Platform."
- **Meta description:** ≤ 155 chars. Promise + differentiator. "Help more people join in and share what they think. Built for teachers, trainers, and team leaders."
- **H1:** Includes a noun phrase that matches user search intent (`anonymous pulse survey`, `live polling for meetings`, `inclusive workshop tool`). Outcome-first.
- **Subhead:** Repeats the primary keyword once, naturally. Or leads with the pillar (Bring people in, Hear every voice, etc.).
