# The organisation as living network: a conceptual map

**The organisation of the future is neither a flatter pyramid nor a leaderless commune — it is a network of small, purpose-aligned, role-based units (human and increasingly agentic AI) operating on transparent information, consent-based decision-making and porous ecosystem boundaries, with formal scaffolding wherever accountability genuinely demands it.** That synthesis is what emerges when the last fifteen years of post-bureaucratic thinking — from Ricardo Semler's maverick Semco through Frédéric Laloux's Teal, Gary Hamel's Humanocracy, Brian Robertson's Holacracy, Haier's Rendanheyi and Jurgen Appelo's unFIX — are laid alongside the 2024–2026 AI-native turn embodied by Ethan Mollick, McKinsey's \"agentic organisation,\" Microsoft's \"Frontier Firm\" and Appelo's own *Human Robot Agent* and \"Networked Agentic Organisation\" (NAO).

These thinkers disagree on metaphysics (consciousness evolution versus economic reform), on mechanics (constitutional legalism versus pattern libraries) and on whether to replace or merely augment hierarchy. But they converge, with striking regularity, on the same ten structural moves — and they share the same failure modes, documented most painfully at Zappos, Spotify and Klarna. What has changed in 2025–2026 is that generative AI is collapsing coordination costs in a way that makes the post-bureaucratic literature newly urgent: the same agentic technology that could finally realise self-management also enables unprecedented algorithmic surveillance, and governance choices — not the technology — decide which arrives.

The sections below trace this intellectual arc in depth: Appelo's continuing evolution, the wider pantheon, the theoretical frameworks they have produced, the cases that prove and disprove them, the AI intersection, and a conceptual blueprint for what the organisation of the future actually looks like.

## From Management 3.0 to the networked agentic organisation

Jurgen Appelo's through-line over fifteen years is that **organisations are complex adaptive living systems, therefore prescriptive frameworks fail, therefore what is needed is a pattern library — a toolbox rather than a constitution**. Everything else flows from that complexity-theoretic commitment.

His 2011 book *Management 3.0: Leading Agile Developers, Developing Agile Leaders* positioned the eponymous movement against two strawmen: Management 1.0 (Taylorist command-and-control) and Management 2.0 (1.0 dressed in Six Sigma, TQM and Balanced Scorecard clothing). Management 3.0 treats the organisation itself as a complex adaptive system, with its mascot \"Martie\" embodying six interrelated views: **energise people, empower teams, align constraints, develop competence, grow structure, improve everything**. Around these views Appelo built a now-canonical toolset — **Moving Motivators** (the CHAMPFROGS ten-card ranking of intrinsic motivators, synthesised from Daniel Pink, Steven Reiss and Self-Determination Theory), **Delegation Poker and its seven symmetrical levels** (Tell, Sell, Consult, Agree, Advise, Inquire, Delegate, extending Hersey–Blanchard to flow both ways), **Kudo Cards** for peer recognition, **Personal Maps** for distributed teams, **Merit Money** for peer-distributed bonuses and the **Celebration Grid** for distinguishing practice-failure from experiment-failure. *Managing for Happiness* (2016) repackaged these as practical workouts.

But Management 3.0 had a structural gap: it told you how to manage, not how to organise. Appelo spent \"twelve years\" looking for the missing scaffold before launching **unFIX** in late 2021 as \"an alternative to SAFe, LeSS, Holacracy, the Spotify model and matrix organisations.\" A crucial clarification: the terms often associated with Appelo — *orbit, tribe, chapter, guild, outpost* — are **not** unFIX vocabulary. *Tribe, chapter* and *guild* are Spotify-model terminology that Appelo explicitly replaced; *orbit* and *outpost* do not appear in the pattern library at all. The actual unFIX taxonomy is built around **Crews** (teams of 3–7), a **Captain** who leads a crew's journey but has no line authority, **Bases** (dozens-to-hundreds of people, analogous to a Spotify tribe but with a single unified management team), **Chiefs** who sit together as one **Governance Crew** and hold all line-management responsibility, **Forums** (cross-crew functional communities with no line management, replacing Spotify chapters and guilds), and **Turfs** (areas cultivated by the same people — codebases, products, districts). Seven **crew types** extend Team Topologies' four (stream-aligned, enabling, complicated-subsystem, platform) with three Appelo additions (governance, experience, partnership). Above the Base, unFIX scales fractally via **Leagues, Clusters, Assemblies, Crowds, Coalitions and Congresses** — though Appelo concedes these upper-level patterns remain hypothetical.

The single most important move in unFIX is the centralisation of all line management in one **Governance Crew of Chiefs** — the anti-matrix move. This directly addresses what Appelo calls the \"malaise in the matrix\" of the Spotify model, where squad members report to separate chapter leads, tribe leads and product owners with different objectives. In unFIX, your *crew* can dissolve, split or merge without your *manager* changing — which operationalises Heidi Helfand's **dynamic reteaming** (teams as assignments drawn from a capability pool, not permanent silos). This marks Appelo's decisive shift from **team-based to capability-based thinking**: people belong to a Base's capability pool, and crews form and dissolve around missions.

The 2024–2026 phase pushes further. Appelo's 2025 book ***Human Robot Agent: New Fundamentals for AI-Driven Leadership with Algorithmic Management*** (endorsed by Agile Manifesto co-authors Jim Highsmith and Alistair Cockburn) extends the complexity-systems frame to include AI agents as organisational participants. His Substack has rebranded as \"**The Solo Chief**,\" aimed at solo operators orchestrating dozens of AI agents. Management 3.0 and unFIX have been merged into **M3K** at m3k.ai, described as \"adaptive networked agentic organisations.\" A new pattern language, **Harmony** (launched September 2025 at harmony.tech), is explicitly designed for human-plus-AI teams and for consumption by AI crawlers themselves — \"holonic without the dogma,\" drawing on Stafford Beer's Viable System Model. The new conceptual vocabulary includes the **Networked Agentic Organisation (NAO)**, positioned as a successor to Teal, DAOs, Holacracy, unFIX and Rendanheyi; the **Solo Chief** archetype; **M-skilled people** (multi-skilled generalists replacing T-shaped specialists); and the **Dual-lane / Ring-road organisation** that separates AI fast tracks from human slow tracks to prevent \"intellectual traffic jams.\"

Appelo positions himself carefully against the competition. On Laloux he is **publicly sceptical**: his post \"There are no teal organisations\" (2016) argues the stages-of-consciousness narrative is an abstraction that people rarely occupy purely. On Robertson he is blunt: Holacracy \"failed because it was too weird, too abstract and too technical.\" Critics in turn note Appelo's **eclecticism** — he self-describes as a \"radical synthesist\" and concedes \"the picture is new; the ideas are not\" — his **neologism inflation** (Crews, Bases, Turfs, Leagues, Clusters, Crowds, Coalitions, Congresses, Domains, Realms), the **absence of peer-reviewed empirical validation** for core claims, and a pivot cadence (M3.0 → unFIX → M3K → Harmony → NAO) that raises consistency concerns.

## The wider pantheon of post-bureaucratic thought

Appelo sits inside a dense intellectual ecology that deserves a comparative map rather than a parade of biographies.

**Frédéric Laloux** remains the field's grand narrator. *Reinventing Organizations* (2014), drawing on Ken Wilber's Integral Theory and Clare Graves's Spiral Dynamics, argues that organisational forms track **stages of human consciousness — Red (impulsive, mafia-like), Amber (conformist, army-like), Orange (achievement, machine-like multinationals), Green (pluralistic, family-like stakeholder firms), and Teal (evolutionary, living organism)**. Teal organisations are defined by three breakthroughs: **self-management, wholeness and evolutionary purpose**. Laloux's case evidence — Buurtzorg, Morning Star, FAVI, Sun Hydraulics, AES, Patagonia, Sounds True — remains the most systematic synthesis of self-managed practice. Since the illustrated edition (2016) he has stepped back from management writing, redirecting energies into **The Week**, a climate-mobilisation project. His framework is empirically contested (developmental-stage ontology is hard to operationalise) but his vocabulary is now load-bearing across the entire field.

**Gary Hamel**, with Michele Zanini, wrote the most *economically* reasoned critique in **Humanocracy** (2020; expanded second edition 2024). Where Laloux is developmental, Hamel is instrumental: bureaucracy is a **$3 trillion annual drag on the US economy** and must be excised rather than reformed. The seven principles of humanocracy — ownership, markets, meritocracy, community, openness, experimentation, paradox — operationalise this. Hamel's **Management Innovation eXchange (MIX)** popularised crowdsourced \"management hacks,\" and his Haier case studies in *Harvard Business Review* (\"The End of Bureaucracy,\" 2018) made **Rendanheyi** internationally legible.

**Brian Robertson's Holacracy** is the field's most *legalistic* form. The **Holacracy Constitution** (currently v5.0, 2021) codifies distributed authority into **roles** (with explicit purpose, domain, accountabilities), **circles** in a holarchy linked by double-linked Lead Links and Rep Links, **tensions** as the raw material of governance, and strictly separated **governance meetings** (using Integrative Decision-Making) from **tactical meetings**. Zappos's disastrous 2015 adoption — Tony Hsieh's \"embrace Holacracy or take a buyout\" ultimatum lost roughly 18 per cent of staff in the first year and pushed Zappos off Fortune's Best Companies list — is the cautionary tale of the century for prescriptive frameworks.

**Aaron Dignan's *Brave New Work*** (2019) and his consultancy **The Ready** (converted to an Employee Ownership Trust in 2025) deliver the field's most widely adopted diagnostic: the **Operating System Canvas** with twelve design surfaces — purpose, authority, structure, strategy, resources, innovation, workflow, meetings, information, membership, mastery, compensation. Dignan is the great synthesist-toolsmith, explicitly standing on Laloux, Hamel, Robertson and Semler.

**Dave Gray's *The Connected Company*** (2012) introduced the vocabulary of **pods** (semi-autonomous, customer-facing units) supported by **platforms** (cultural, technical and procedural infrastructure that supports without controlling). This pod-plus-platform architecture is the direct ancestor of Spotify squads, Appelo crews and Haier microenterprises. *Gamestorming* (2010) gave the movement its visual-facilitation grammar.

**Ricardo Semler** is the necessary historical maverick. *Maverick!* (1993) and *The Seven-Day Weekend* (2003) describe Semco's industrial democracy decades before Teal vocabulary existed: employees setting their own salaries, voting on their bosses, eliminating HR, open-book management. Semler provides no framework — just iconoclastic stories — yet every subsequent thinker cites Semco as foundational.

**Dee Hock**, founder of VISA, coined **\"chaordic\"** (chaos plus order) in *Birth of the Chaordic Age* (1999). Chaordic organisations operate on simple shared purpose and principles (\"complex rules produce simple, stupid behaviour; simple principles produce complex, intelligent behaviour\") with distributed ownership. VISA itself — 20,000 member institutions, hundreds of millions of customers, no central owner — is the proto-platform organisation and a direct ancestor of today's DAO thinking.

A fresher layer of 2018–2026 voices extends the pantheon. **Matt K. Parker's *A Radical Enterprise*** (2022) translates self-management into four imperatives (team autonomy, managerial devolution, deficiency gratification, candid vulnerability) in DevOps-friendly vocabulary. **Samantha Slade's *Going Horizontal*** (2018) offers a seven-practice incremental on-ramp for organisations unwilling to adopt Holacracy wholesale. **Lisa Gill's *Leadermorphosis*** podcast has, through 100-plus episodes, become the field's main curator and community hub. **Corporate Rebels** (Joost Minnaar and Pim de Morree) have turned field visits into a platform, eight \"bucket list\" shifts and an annual summit. **Amy Edmondson's psychological safety** research (*The Fearless Organization*, 2018; *Right Kind of Wrong*, 2023) supplies the behavioural *precondition* that every structural theorist tacitly assumes. **Stanley McChrystal's *Team of Teams*** (2015) — built on the Joint Special Operations Task Force's reinvention against Al-Qaeda in Iraq — pairs **shared consciousness** (radical information transparency) with **empowered execution** (push decisions to the edge) and lent the movement military legitimacy. **John Kotter's Dual Operating System** (*Accelerate*, 2014) offers the conservative compromise: keep the hierarchy, bolt on a volunteer strategy network alongside it. **Henry Mintzberg's 2023 *Understanding Organizations… Finally!*** delivers the pluralist corrective, updating his classic typology to four dominant forms (Personal Enterprise, Programmed Machine, Professional Assembly, Project Pioneer/Adhocracy) and insisting the single-best-form assumption buried in Teal, Humanocracy and Holacracy is a category error. At the frontier, **Kevin Owocki** (Gitcoin), and academic voices such as **Ian Khan, Michael Lustenberger and Florian Spychiger**, are beginning to theorise **DAOs and AI-native organisations** as computational extensions of Hock's chaordic logic.

## The theoretical frameworks, clarified

Beneath the thinkers sits a family of frameworks whose mechanics and genealogies deserve precision.

**Self-management** traces to Mary Parker Follett's \"power-with\" (1920s), Tavistock's socio-technical systems (Trist and Bamforth, 1951) and Drucker's knowledge-worker thesis (*Landmarks of Tomorrow*, 1959). It is not \"leaderless\" but *many-leadered* — authority migrates from position to expertise and context. **Distributed authority** works through two canonical decision protocols: Laloux's **advice process** (anyone can decide anything, but must first consult everyone meaningfully affected and the relevant experts; advice must be considered, not followed), and sociocratic **consent** (\"good enough for now, safe enough to try\" — no paramount reasoned objection required, unlike consensus).

**Network organisations** are theorised by Manuel Castells's *The Rise of the Network Society* (1996) and grounded in Mark Granovetter's 1973 \"Strength of Weak Ties\" and Ronald Burt's 1992 structural holes. The claim: in turbulent environments, networks beat hierarchies because they are flexible, scalable and resilient to node failure.

**Sociocracy** is Dutch — **Kees Boeke** at the Children's Community Workshop (1945), **Gerard Endenburg** at his family electrical firm (fully implemented 1984) — and rests on four principles: consent governs policy, circles are semi-autonomous, circles are double-linked (two representatives between levels), and roles are filled by open consent elections. **Sociocracy 3.0**, released under Creative Commons by Bernhard Bockelbrink, James Priest and Liliana David (2015), deliberately disassembles sociocracy into a **library of seventy-plus patterns** adoptable one at a time — closer in philosophy to Appelo's pattern-library approach than to Robertson's constitutional one.

**Holacracy** is the most codified form: tensions, roles (not jobs), circles in a holarchy, Integrative Decision-Making, governance meetings separated from tactical meetings, the Glassfrog software as record-keeper. Teal organisations, operationalised, use the same components Laloux distilled from his cases: advice process, peer-set compensation (Morning Star's colleague committees, FAVI's peer review), explicit conflict-resolution protocols (Buurtzorg's four-step process), check-in rituals, \"empty chair\" for the organisation's own voice.

**Dynamic teaming** comes from Heidi Helfand's *Dynamic Reteaming* (2018; 2nd ed. 2020), which rejects the Tuckman model as aspirational and offers five reteaming patterns: one-by-one, grow-and-split, isolation, merging and switching. **Team Topologies** (Matthew Skelton and Manuel Pais, 2019) has become the canonical software-team taxonomy — four team types (**stream-aligned, enabling, complicated-subsystem, platform**) interacting via three modes (**collaboration, X-as-a-service, facilitating**), with cognitive load as the governing constraint. **Platform organisations**, theorised by Parker, Van Alstyne and Choudary's *Platform Revolution* (2016), and **ecosystem organisations**, theorised by James Moore's *The Death of Competition* (1996) and Iansiti and Levien's *The Keystone Advantage* (2004), extend organisational logic beyond firm boundaries — keystones, niche players and orchestrators replace the integrated firm. **DAOs**, running on blockchain smart contracts with token-weighted governance, are the computational frontier: their track record (MakerDAO, Uniswap, Gitcoin) is real, but so are their failure modes — plutocracy, low voter turnout under ten per cent, hostile takeovers such as the Build Finance DAO drain of February 2022.

## How these frameworks break with traditional hierarchy

The contrast with the **functional Weberian hierarchy** — unity of command, span of control, job descriptions, career ladders, budget allocation through chain-of-command, information asymmetry as a managerial tool — is categorical rather than incremental.

Traditional hierarchies optimise for **predictability and compliance**; post-bureaucratic forms optimise for **adaptation and contribution**. Hierarchies route decisions upward to where authority is concentrated; these frameworks route decisions toward wherever expertise and tension are sensed. Hierarchies assign **jobs** (stable clusters of tasks codified in descriptions); these frameworks assign **roles** (purposes, domains, accountabilities) that people hold in portfolios of three to seven at a time. Hierarchies pool capital and allocate it top-down; Haier's Rendanheyi and Morning Star's CLOUs create **internal markets** where units buy and sell from each other and compensation tracks external value delivered. Hierarchies treat information as a managerial prerogative; these frameworks treat transparency as the default — Buffer publishes every salary, GitLab publishes a 2,700-page handbook, Haier exposes every microenterprise's P&L. Hierarchies presume stable departments drawn on an org chart; these frameworks presume **fluid crews** forming and dissolving around missions, mapped as capability graphs rather than boxes.

The deeper philosophical break concerns the **metaphor of the organisation itself**. Taylorist hierarchy is a machine; post-bureaucratic thought treats the organisation as a living, complex adaptive system — with the corollary that it cannot be controlled, only cultivated. Hamel's humanocracy is the most economically pointed version; Laloux's Teal the most developmentally rich; Dee Hock's chaordic the oldest and most philosophical. They all share the rejection of control-engineering in favour of purpose-and-principles-plus-emergence.

## What the cases actually prove — and disprove

The implementation record is mixed, and honesty about it is the most useful contribution research can make.

**Buurtzorg** is the clearest positive case: founded by Jos de Blok in 2006 in Almelo, it now runs some **900 self-managing nursing teams of up to twelve**, serving roughly 80,000 Dutch patients annually with an overhead of about **eight per cent versus the sector's twenty-five per cent**, and using around forty per cent fewer care hours per patient than traditional providers (KPMG, 2015). Staff satisfaction sits at 9.5 out of 10. Yet Buurtzorg has also faced near-insolvency during insurer tariff disputes (2015), and international replications have struggled with different reimbursement systems — and de Blok's charismatic founder role is load-bearing in ways the \"self-managing\" branding obscures.

**Haier's Rendanheyi** — **roughly 4,000 microenterprises** of 10–15 people within an 80,000-person workforce, generating over $35 billion in revenue and having absorbed GE Appliances in 2016 — is the largest-scale evidence that self-management can scale. Zhang Ruimin eliminated some 12,000 middle-management positions beginning in 2013. Each ME operates on an **EMC contract** (binding targets against the best global competitor), user-paid salaries and internal buy-sell relationships with \"Node MEs\" providing shared services. But implementation rigour varies enormously across MEs, workers face income volatility unfamiliar to Western labour markets, and cultural transplantation outside China remains unproven.

**Morning Star** — the California tomato processor founded by Chris Rufer, handling roughly forty per cent of US processed-tomato volume with about 400 year-round colleagues and no managers — demonstrates that **Colleague Letters of Understanding** (annually negotiated commitments with seven to twelve internal customers and suppliers) can replace job descriptions in continuous-process manufacturing. But new hires frequently leave in year one (research by Paul Green Jr.), and ultimate ownership by a single founder-owner complicates the \"no bosses\" claim.

**Patagonia** is not a self-management case but a **governance innovation**: in September 2022, Yvon Chouinard's family transferred 100 per cent of voting shares to the Patagonia Purpose Trust and 98 per cent of non-voting shares to the Holdfast Collective (a 501(c)(4) nonprofit), committing all distributed profits to environmental causes in perpetuity. Through 2025 this generated roughly $180 million in Holdfast donations. Critics note the arrangement was largely tax-free, that family influence persists without independent oversight, and that Patagonia operationally remains a conventional hierarchy.

**W. L. Gore** has run a **lattice organisation** for sixty-five years: no titles, sponsors instead of supervisors, commitments rather than assignments, peer-based ranking, and — crucially — a cap of about 150 people per facility (echoing Dunbar's number) with new plants spun off in co-located campuses. The model has produced sustained innovation (Gore-Tex, Elixir strings, medical devices) and roughly $5 billion in revenue, but takes new associates about 18 months to become productive and obscures power concentrations among long-tenured associates.

**Spotify's \"tribes, squads, chapters and guilds\" model** is the field's most important *negative* case. Henrik Kniberg and Anders Ivarsson's 2012 whitepaper described an *aspirational snapshot* that Spotify itself never fully implemented. **Jeremiah Lee's 2020 \"Failed #SquadGoals\"** essay, written from inside Spotify, documents the reality: matrix management via multiple chapter leads, fragmentation from excessive autonomy, dependency chaos between squads, insufficient coaching, inconsistent tooling. Kniberg himself has repeatedly said \"this is a snapshot, not a recipe.\" Yet ING, BT and many banks cargo-culted the vocabulary without the culture, producing renamed departments and no behavioural change. The pattern is general: **structure without culture is costume, not transformation**.

**Zappos's Holacracy adoption** (2013–2020) is the largest experiment and the clearest failure. Tony Hsieh's March 2015 \"embrace Holacracy or leave\" memo triggered an immediate 14 per cent departure, rising to roughly 18 per cent by early 2016. The organisation reorganised into around 500 circles and 7,500 roles. By 2020, Aimee Groth's Quartz reporting confirmed Zappos had quietly abandoned most Holacracy practices, keeping \"circular\" structures while reintroducing managers and rebranding as \"Market-Based Dynamics\" and \"Teal.\"

**Valve's flat structure** — 100 per cent self-allocated time, literal desks on wheels, cabals forming around shared goals — remains famously profitable per employee and powers Steam, the dominant PC-games platform. But Jeri Ellsworth's post-firing 2013 testimony and the People Make Games 2024 investigation describe \"Lord of the Flies\" dynamics, a \"hidden layer of powerful management\" and in-group favouritism. This is Jo Freeman's 1970 \"Tyranny of Structurelessness\" playing out in real time: informal hierarchies form wherever formal ones are abolished.

**Buffer** demonstrated that radical salary transparency (public from December 2013) can **boost applications by 229 per cent** — but also that transparency brands don't prevent bad planning: in June 2016 Buffer laid off 11 per cent of staff when salaries reached 80 per cent of operating expenses. **GitLab's handbook-first, all-remote model** (over 2,700 public pages, 2,000-plus employees in 60-plus countries, IPO 2021) has become the de facto reference for distributed work, though its local-rate compensation system remains contested.

The empirical pattern across these cases is unambiguous: **self-management works where culture, ownership structure and founder commitment align, and fails where it is imposed prescriptively or where informal power is allowed to fill the vacuum left by formal abolition**.

## The AI inflection: agentic organisations and the great flattening

The 2024–2026 discourse has moved decisively from \"will AI boost productivity?\" to \"what is the shape of the firm when intelligence is abundant and coordination costs collapse?\" Five strands of evidence are converging.

First, **AI-native startups are demonstrating radical organisational compression**. Cursor reached roughly $300M ARR with twelve employees; 11x, Cognition and Crosby Legal operate as \"agentic\" go-to-market and legal firms. The a16z/Gennaro Cuofano archetypes — the Two-Layer Structure, the Slime Mold Network, the Super IC — describe firms where middle management is effectively deleted and a thin leadership layer orchestrates autonomous individual contributors augmented by agent swarms. Sam Altman's group-chat bet that the first one-person billion-dollar company arrives between 2026 and 2028 is extrapolation, not fact, but the trajectory is real.

Second, **the mainstream consultancies have crystallised the agentic-organisation frame**. McKinsey's December 2025 pieces \"The agentic organisation: contours of the next paradigm\" and \"Accountability by design in the agentic organisation\" argue that org charts pivot toward **work charts** — networks of agentic teams exchanging tasks — where \"one manager with a small human team could orchestrate hundreds of agents running 24/7.\" Only six per cent of firms qualify as \"AI high performers\" (over five per cent of EBIT from AI), and the differentiator is organisational, not technological. Microsoft's April 2025 Work Trend Index introduced the **Frontier Firm** and the **\"agent boss\"** — every employee managing a portfolio of digital teammates. IBM's AskHR agent automates 94 per cent of routine HR tasks; its AskIT agent generated a reported $3.5 billion productivity gain.

Third, **middle management is being hollowed out at scale**. Gartner projects that 20 per cent of organisations will use AI to eliminate at least half their middle-management positions by 2026. Middle-management postings fell 42 per cent from 2022 to end-2024. US managers now oversee three times the staff they did in 2015. Amazon's Andy Jassy mandated a 15 per cent increase in IC-to-manager ratios by Q1 2025 and announced roughly 14,000 corporate-role eliminations tied to \"efficiency gains and AI integration.\" Google cut VP/manager roles by 10 per cent in 2024; Intel cut roughly half of management; Citigroup plans a 20,000-headcount reduction for 2026 citing middle-office automation. Challenger, Gray & Christmas counted 946,000 US job-cut announcements through October 2025, the highest since the 2020 pandemic peak.

Fourth, **the cautionary tales have caught up with the euphoria**. Tobi Lütke's April 2025 Shopify memo made \"reflexive AI usage a baseline expectation\" and required teams to prove AI *cannot* do the work before requesting headcount — backed by roughly 24 MCP servers and an internal LLM proxy built over years. Klarna's CEO Sebastian Siemiatkowski publicly walked back the firm's \"AI replaces 700 agents\" story in May 2025: \"cost unfortunately seems to have been a too predominant evaluation factor… we went too far.\" Klarna began rehiring customer-service humans in an \"Uber-style\" flexible pool. Duolingo's Luis von Ahn backpedalled on his \"AI-first\" memo within weeks after contractor and user backlash, eventually removing AI usage as a formal performance metric in 2026. The deeper pattern: **CEO bravado memos, worker pushback, partial retreat — but the underlying structural change continues**.

Fifth, **the theory of the firm is being rewritten**. A 2025 NBER paper (\"The Coasean Singularity?\" by Hadfield, Koh and collaborators) argues that AI agents collapse transaction costs toward zero, potentially dissolving Coase's 1937 rationale for firm boundaries. *California Management Review's* April 2025 counterpoint — \"From Coase to AI Agents\" — notes that AI can also raise internal entropy and create platform lock-ins, so firms may not shrink linearly. A 2026 arXiv preprint, \"The Headless Firm,\" argues agentic AI shifts coordination from a combinatorial-edge regime to a throughput-linear one. Appelo's own formulation is the **capability graph**, where humans and AI agents are nodes with explicit capabilities and crews assemble dynamically around problems.

The central tension is that **AI simultaneously centralises and decentralises**. Cheap intelligence empowers individual contributors, enables solopreneurs, shrinks firm boundaries and supports self-management with real-time sensing; but proprietary data becomes moat, algorithmic management enables granular surveillance, and LLM infrastructure concentrates in a handful of vendors. EU Parliament data (2025) show 74 per cent of US employers use online tracking, 61 per cent deploy AI-powered productivity analytics and 27 per cent of managers admit that worker mental and physical health is not adequately protected under algorithmic management. Governance choices — not the technology — will decide whether the agentic firm realises Laloux's self-management or Taylor's surveillance.

## Ten patterns, eleven failure modes

When Management 3.0, unFIX, Teal, Humanocracy, Holacracy, Sociocracy, Rendanheyi, The Connected Company, Brave New Work, Team of Teams and Team Topologies are laid side by side, ten shared structural moves appear with striking consistency:

1. **Decentralisation of authority** — pushing decisions to where tension and expertise actually sit.
2. **Purpose as North Star** — evolutionary purpose (Laloux), zero distance to user (Haier), community (Hamel) — not shareholder primacy.
3. **Roles over jobs** — portfolios of three to seven roles per person rather than a single job description.
4. **Transparency as default** — open financials, open salaries, visible P&L per unit.
5. **Consent or advice over command** — good-enough-for-now decisions; no paramount reasoned objections.
6. **Dynamic, fluid structures** — teams as assignments, not silos; structure continuously refactored.
7. **Small units, nested** — crews of 5–9, departments of 40–60, facilities capped near Dunbar's 150.
8. **Value-stream orientation** — customer journeys replace functional silos as the primary unit.
9. **Psychological safety as precondition** — Edmondson's substrate beneath every structural claim.
10. **Continuous learning and experimentation** — the organisation as ongoing experiment, not finished machine.

Layered over these in 2025–2026 is an emerging **eleventh** pattern: **AI augmentation baked in at the operating-system level**, not bolted on as tooling.

The failure modes are equally consistent and worth stating plainly. Jo Freeman's 1970 **\"Tyranny of Structurelessness\"** remains the single most predictive diagnosis: absent formal hierarchy, informal hierarchies form around social capital, media access and founder proximity — visible at Valve, in Occupy and in early GitHub. **Scaling breakdown past Dunbar's 150** recurs across cases; Gore's facility-size cap is the best response we have. **Prescriptive imposition collapses self-management** — the Zappos-Hsieh ultimatum violated Holacracy's own ideals and accelerated exit. **Cargo-culting structure without culture** produces agile theatre: renamed squads, ritualised standups, zero behavioural change. **Founder dependence and purpose drift** haunt post-founder transitions at Zappos and threaten Buurtzorg, Morning Star and Patagonia's long-run durability. **Consent-based slowness** can make decisions crawl. **Conflict avoidance and accountability gaps** emerge when no one has to confront underperformance. **Incompatibility with public-market and regulatory reporting** pushes self-managed firms toward private, cooperative or steward ownership. **Unclear career ladders** — only 6 per cent of Gen Z aspires to senior leadership (Deloitte 2024/25) — leave self-managed firms struggling to articulate growth. **The autonomy–alignment tension** is canonical and permanent. And specific to the AI era: **the Klarna arc** (over-automating empathy-heavy work), **the Duolingo arc** (crude AI-usage mandates), the **collapse of the management development pipeline** (Fortune's April 2026 case of a VP with 47 direct reports after layoffs), and **algorithmic-management dehumanisation**.

## A conceptual blueprint for the organisation of the future

Synthesising the theory and the cases, a coherent design emerges. Call it the **Augmented Network Organisation**. Its ten constituent elements are these.

**Evolutionary purpose as North Star** provides alignment where KPI trees cannot; humans and AI continuously re-sense the environment and re-interpret the purpose. **Nested small units** — crews of 5–9, bases or tribes of 40–60, facilities or divisions capped near 150 before deliberate splitting — operationalise Dunbar, Gore and Haier at fractal scale. **Networks of teams** replace hierarchies of teams: Team Topologies' four team types extended with agentic teams per McKinsey's work charts, connected by explicit interaction modes. **Consent and advice decision-making, accelerated by AI** — AI agents compress the Laloux advice step from weeks to hours by summarising stakeholder input, surfacing risks and simulating outcomes, directly attacking the \"slow consent\" failure mode.

**Radical information transparency, engineered rather than aspirational** — every unit's P&L visible, every salary public where law permits, handbooks public by default. AI becomes the vector of *information democracy*, giving every employee on-demand access to data previously hoarded by managers. **Role-based, not job-based work**, with role portfolios reassigned quarterly and a skills or capability graph serving as the primary coordination artefact instead of an org chart. **Appelo's capability graph** extended to include humans *and* AI agents as nodes with explicit capability profiles, so crews form dynamically — the Coasean collapse operationalised without fragmenting identity. **Human-plus-AI augmentation as a first-class design problem**, with explicit separation of **fast tracks** (agent-speed, 24/7, machine-auditable) from **slow tracks** (human deliberation, ethics, stakeholder trust) per Appelo's dual-lane framing.

**Platform and ecosystem boundaries, deliberately porous** — the firm becomes a platform providing shared services (legal, data, brand, compute) on which internal microenterprises, contractors and even customers co-create, with external agents negotiating with internal agents via standardised protocols. Shopify's roughly 24 MCP servers illustrate this platform layer at infrastructure depth. **Continuous experimentation with clear kill criteria** — Hamel's experimentation principle, McChrystal's shared-consciousness loops, with AI lowering experiment costs via simulation and synthetic data.

The design requires explicit **counterweights to the known failure modes**: formalise hierarchy where fiduciary, regulatory or safety accountability genuinely demands named individuals (Freeman's remedy); cap unit size at Dunbar thresholds (Gore's remedy); invest in coaching and peer-mentoring roles to replace the developmental pipeline that middle-management elimination has broken; govern algorithmic management actively under NIST AI RMF and EU AI Act principles — transparency, worker voice, right to explanation; protect slow tracks from agent-speed pressure to prevent the \"infinite workday\" Microsoft has documented; and design purpose continuity through EOTs, steward-ownership or cooperatives that survive founder transitions.

## What has actually changed

The frameworks of the last fifteen years were compelling diagnoses whose adoption curve was limited by a coordination-cost ceiling: self-managed organisations generated more decisions, more meetings and more information than human coordination could cheaply absorb past 150 people. What 2024–2026 has added is a technology — agentic AI — that selectively collapses those coordination costs, and a theoretical apparatus (McKinsey's agentic organisation, Microsoft's Frontier Firm, Appelo's NAO and Harmony, Hadfield's Coasean Singularity) that frames the collapse honestly. **The result is not that post-bureaucratic frameworks have been vindicated, but that their ceiling has been raised, while their failure modes have been sharpened by new risks — algorithmic surveillance, management-pipeline collapse, empathy deficits in over-automated customer work.**

The honest conclusion is pluralist. Mintzberg's corrective stands: different contexts demand different forms. Public-market fiduciary requirements, safety-critical domains and heavily regulated industries will retain more hierarchy for good reasons. But for knowledge-intensive, customer-facing, fast-changing work — which is a growing share of the economy — the Augmented Network Organisation is the direction of travel. The books worth reading in order, for anyone building such a firm, remain: Semler (*Maverick!*, 1993) for the maverick temperament; Hock (*Birth of the Chaordic Age*, 1999) for the philosophical base; Gray (*The Connected Company*, 2012) for the pod-plus-platform pattern; Laloux (*Reinventing Organizations*, 2014) for the grand narrative and case evidence; Robertson (*Holacracy*, 2015) for the constitutional mechanics; McChrystal (*Team of Teams*, 2015) for legitimacy at scale; Edmondson (*The Fearless Organization*, 2018) for the behavioural substrate; Dignan (*Brave New Work*, 2019) for the canvas; Skelton and Pais (*Team Topologies*, 2019) for the technical taxonomy; Hamel and Zanini (*Humanocracy*, 2020) for the economic argument; Parker (*A Radical Enterprise*, 2022) for a DevOps-era translation; Mintzberg (*Understanding Organizations… Finally!*, 2023) for the pluralist corrective; Mollick (*Co-Intelligence*, 2024) and Hoffman (*Superagency*, 2025) for the AI-partnership frame; and Appelo (*Human Robot Agent*, 2025) for the synthesis of pattern-library thinking with agentic AI.

The unifying claim across all of it is simple and hard: **organisations are living networks, not machines; management is design, not control; and the work of the next decade is to build firms — both human and agentic — worthy of the people inside them.**
